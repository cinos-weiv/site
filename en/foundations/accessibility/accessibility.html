Accessibility

Start designing with accessibility in mind by first understanding users' real-life situations and needs.

Accessibility design is a strategy to improve product quality and ensure equitable use. When done well, users may not even notice who it specifically benefits — they simply experience a design that feels usable, welcoming, and accessible to everyone.

 

Starting from Accessibility

In real educational and administrative settings, roles like students, teachers, school administrators, and IT staff may face limitations. These can include reading difficulties, temporary hand injuries, age-related hearing loss, or simply harsh lighting or screen glare. All of which affect how users interact with products.

For example:

  • Students may encounter reading disabilities or rely on keyboard-accessible assistive tools when using digital learning materials.
  • Teachers often need to switch screens, write, and click rapidly during lectures. If feedback is unclear or buttons are too small, it can create operational stress.
  • School administrators must evaluate whether the system fairly provides equal participation opportunities to every user.
  • IT managers need to ensure that devices and platforms support assistive technologies and can be maintained reliably.

As our products expand into business environments, accessibility continues to play a crucial role.

Whether it’s remote collaboration, conferencing systems, or display tools, failing to consider user constraints and flexibility can reduce the overall experience and adoption.

Accessibility is one of the core values of the ViewSonic Design System. We continue integrating it into component styles, token definitions, and handoff documentation.

While most use cases today are from education, we’re starting to build accessibility by default into business and collaboration products, ensuring all users can interact smoothly.

Accessibility is not designed for just a few — it's meant to ensure everyone can use our products under all conditions.

It’s not just a legal requirement. It’s a fundamental responsibility that improves product quality and user experience.

 

Statistics show:

  • Around 300 to 350 million people worldwide are affected by color vision deficiency, approximately 4.5% of the population — including about 8% of men.
  • About 10–20% of the population have language-based learning disabilities (such as dyslexia), meaning 1 in 10 people in educational settings may be affected.
  • Approximately 430 million people worldwide have moderate or severe hearing loss, including 34 million children.
  • At least 2.2 billion people experience some form of vision impairment, with over 1 billion lacking proper treatment.
For full data, refer to the source links below.

 

What is Accessibility?

Accessibility is a set of design principles that ensure everyone, regardless of ability, age, or environment, can access, understand, and interact with digital content.

Common Aspects of Accessibility Design:

  • Visual: color contrast, alt text, clear typography, screen reader support.
  • Auditory: captions, transcripts, visual cues.
  • Cognitive: simplified language, clear structure, reduced distractions.
  • Interaction: keyboard navigation, properly labeled fields, logical focus order.

 

Design with accessibility in mind from the start

You don’t need to meet every standard all at once. Instead, begin by considering limitations and usage contexts early in the design process. This approach will lead to more accessible products in practice.

Begin with some simple checks:

  • Is the text clear? Is the color contrast sufficient?
  • Do the images include alt text?
  • Can all interactions be done via keyboard or touch?
  • Can users understand how to navigate and respond to error messages?
For a full list of checklist items, see the next chapter: Accessibility Checklist.

 

Accessibility standards we follow

When designing and developing educational products, it is essential to include specific accessibility features to meet U.S. legal requirements. This ensures that all students, especially those with disabilities, can fully participate in the learning experience.

These requirements are not optional. They are mandated by the following regulations:

  • ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act): Requires that digital content and tools are accessible to individuals with disabilities, ensuring equal access to resources.
  • Section 504 (of the Rehabilitation Act): Mandates that all schools receiving federal funding provide reasonable accommodations and support.
  • IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act): Ensures that students with disabilities receive appropriate education in the least restrictive environment.
  • WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines): Defines accessibility standards for web and application content. WCAG 2.1 AA is commonly considered the minimum benchmark.

How we validate accessibility design

You don’t need to memorize all accessibility standards during the design and development process. However, you should be familiar with basic checklist items and testing methods and plan room for validation.

Recommended validation steps:

Design system level

Used when planning or maintaining a design system:

  • Ensure that all Color Tokens meet the recommended contrast ratio between text and background.
  • Text size should support optimal readability. Body text is recommended to be no smaller than 16px, or adjusted according to platform or device.
  • Make sure that components using Alias Tokens have passed color and text contrast testing.
  • All interactive elements (e.g., Buttons, Checkboxes) should have sufficient tap or click area, with a minimum of 44px × 44px unless otherwise specified.
  • Every component should have a focus style, with an outline contrast ratio of at least 3:1, to support keyboard navigation and users with high contrast needs.
Tips: Recommendations for sizes and units
All dimension-related values (e.g., 16px, 44px) are based on CSS pixels (CSS px).
For iOS or Android development, convert these units to pt or dp.

 

UI mockup level

Used for checking UI mockups before handoff:

  • Ensure components are clearly arranged with adequate spacing and line height for readability.
  • Avoid relying solely on color or icons (e.g., red warning symbols); include supporting text explanations.
  • Error messages should clearly state the cause and how to resolve it (e.g., "Please enter a valid email address").

 

Accessibility validation support documents

Used when preparing for accessibility validation, compliance checks, or syncing with the development team:

  • Provide annotated focus order (aligned with WCAG’s "Operable" principle), recommending a sequence that follows natural screen reader logic.
  • Offer suggested screen reader labels, indicating which elements require aria-label or descriptive text.

 

Common accessibility testing tools:

  • Stark (Figma Plugin): Checks color contrast and text legibility in design files.
  • A11y - Focus Order (Figma Plugin): Helps annotate and review focus order in interactive flows.
  • VoiceOver (macOS) / NVDA (Windows): Simulates real screen reader experiences. Recommended to test alongside accessibility annotations.
  • Axe ／ WAVE ／ Lighthouse: Performs comprehensive audits of developed pages, covering structure, operability, and color contrast.
  • VPAT Template: Helps track and document compliance with common accessibility standards, especially for large-scale projects or handoffs.
